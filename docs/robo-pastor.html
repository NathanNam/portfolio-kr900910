Can Machines Help Christians Make Ethical Decisions?

Keywords

robo-pastor, Biblical interpretation, NLP, clustering, summarization

Need to summarize different Biblical interpretations

Many Christians try to make ethical decisions based on their interpretations of the Bible. Some have the right educational and social backgrounds to make such decisions themselves, but most seek the advice of pastors. However, there are some cases where talking directly to pastors to answer ethical questions is limited or even dangerous. For example, consider a gay Christian teenager boy who wants to know what the Bible says about his sexuality. If a pastor at his church detests homosexuality, listening to such pastor’s Biblical interpretation about homosexuality might be devastating to the boy, and it can even make him leave Christianity. Since a specific Biblical interpretation can have a profound impact on Christians’ lives, Christians including pastors need an efficient way to summarize how different people interpret the Bible for a given ethical question to avoid narrow-minded viewpoints. If a machine can aggregate different biblical interpretations available online and provide advice for a given issue with proper references to the Bible, the machine can be a robo-pastor and help Christians make better ethical decisions.

Using NLP for robo-pastor

The robo-pastor will be a bot that uses machine learning techniques and natural language processing. An input to the robo-pastor will be a yes/no question like, “Should I love my neighbor?”. The robo-pastor will generate three outputs. The first is a verse or a chapter from the Bible that is the most relevant to the question asked. For the above question, this first output might look like, “Love your neighbor as yourself (Mark 12:31)”. The second output will be a percentage of interpretations that will say “Yes” to the question asked and the key sentence of “Yes” interpretations. For the above example, this output will look like “98% Yes - God wants you to love your neighbor”. The last output will be similar to the second output, but it will be for “No”. This output for our example will look like “2% No – it’s okay to hate your neighbor”.

Figure 3: System Architecture Diagram

To generate outputs from a given question, the robo-pastor will use the input question to find out a relevant topic from pre-defined topic list. For our example, the topic will be “love neighbor”. Then, the robo-pastor will look for the most relevant verse or chapter from the Bible tagged with the given topic. Once such verse or chapter is found, the robo-pastor will look in the database to find out interpretations tagged with the selected verse or chapter. For each interpretation found, the robo-pastor will summarize it in one sentence and tag the sentence as “Yes” or “No” based on its relationship to the topic we first found. Then, the robo-pastor will calculate “Yes” and “No” percentages and select key sentences for “Yes” and “No” which best summarize each answer.

Next steps for robo-pastor

As described above, the robo-pastor summarizes different biblical interpretations on a given issue in a simple “Yes/No” output with a specific reference to the Bible, and this will help Christians make faster ethical decisions without having to rely on one source of interpretation. However, there is a very obvious limitation for the robo-pastor: a simple “Yes” or “No” answer might not be the correct answer for the given user. If the robo-pastor can understand different situations of a user and make more intricate advice, it will truly help Christians make better ethical decisions and bring the Second Reformation in our history.
